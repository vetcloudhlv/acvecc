📖Definitions

TermDefinitionUnitsKey Note
Osmolality Number of osmoles per mass of solvent mOsm/kg Depends on temperature & pressure of solvent
Osmolarity Number of osmoles per volume of solvent mOsm/L Independent of temperature/pressure
Osmole 1 mole of any fully dissociated substance dissolved in water — Measured by freezing point depression osmometry
Tonicity Osmotic pressure between two compartments; based on effective osmoles — Na⁺ is main determinant; glucose <10 mOsm/kg normally
Reflection coefficient (σ) Measure of membrane permeability to a solute 0–1 σ=0 fully permeable; σ=1 fully selective. BBB: high. Hepatic sinusoids: low
⚠️ Key Rule
Hypernatremia = hypertonicity BUT hyponatremia ≠ necessarily hypotonicity

⚗️Osmoles & Osmole Gap

Effective Osmoles

Cannot freely cross cell membrane → exert osmotic pressure → determine tonicity

Na⁺ K⁺ Glucose

Ineffective Osmoles

Freely cross cell membrane → no osmotic pressure

BUN Ethylene glycol Ethanol Methanol Aspirin
🔴 Osmole Gap
Measured osmolality should NOT exceed calculated by >10 mOsm/kg. If it does → unmeasured solute present (mannitol, ethylene glycol) or pseudohyponatraemia (hyperproteinaemia / hyperlipidaemia)

🧠Osmoregulation

Two major mechanisms controlling plasma osmolality (changes <1%):

1. ADH (Antidiuretic Hormone)

Stimuli for ADH Release
  • A) Increasing plasma osmolality — osmoreceptors in hypothalamus
  • B) Reduced effective circulating volume (ECV) — baroreceptors More Potent

ADH Mechanism

ADH released
→
Binds V2 receptor on basolateral membrane of collecting tubule
↓
AQ2 channels inserted into luminal membrane of collecting duct
→
Water reabsorbed into tubular cell
Other ADH Effects:
  • ↑ Na reabsorption & K⁺ excretion in collecting ducts
  • ↑ Cl reabsorption in loop of Henle
  • ↑ Urea reabsorption in inner medullary collecting duct
  • Release of vWF from Weibel-Palade bodies
Other stimuli: Nausea, pain/stress, hypoglycaemia, opioids

2. Thirst

Acts as a complementary mechanism to ADH in maintaining osmolality.

🔵Hyponatremia

Core Mechanism
Almost always = free water retention in excess of sodium retention ± concurrent sodium loss
Prevalence
Most common electrolyte disorder: 26% Dogs · 50% Cats

Classification by Tonicity → Volume Status

1. Hypotonic MOST COMMON
→ Water balance impairment | Treat with fluid within 10 mEq/L of patient's Na⁺
2. Isotonic (Pseudohyponatremia)
Artifact (flame photometry) | Hyperlipidaemia, Hyperproteinaemia
3. Hypertonic (Dilutional)
Exogenous osmoles ↑ tonicity → dilutional ↓ Na | Hyperglycaemia, Mannitol
Hypotonic Hyponatremia — Subclassified by Volume Status:
HYPOVOLEMIC

Mechanism: ↓ ECV → non-osmotic ADH (baroreceptors)

UNa <30, UOsm >200:
  • GI loss
  • Third space
  • Cutaneous loss
UNa >30, UOsm >200:
  • Hypoadrenocorticism
  • Loop diuretics
  • Cerebral salt wasting
EUVOLEMIC

Mechanism: Inappropriate ADH / impaired water excretion

Tx: Stop water; give furosemide
UNa <30, UOsm ≤200:
  • Psychogenic polydipsia
  • Hypotonic fluid infusion
UNa >30, UOsm >200:
  • SIADH (intracranial/pulmonary disease, drugs, seizures, tumours)
  • Hypothyroidism
  • Thiazide diuretics
  • Potassium depletion
HYPERVOLEMIC

Mechanism: ↑ TBW but inadequate water excretion or ↓ ECV

UNa <30, UOsm >200:
  • Heart failure (untreated)
  • Cirrhosis
UNa >30, UOsm >200:
  • Kidney failure (AKI)
  • Nephrotic Syndrome
Hypertonic Hyponatremia — Glucose Correction
  • Glucose <400 mg/dL → Na⁺ decreases 1.6 mEq/L per 100 mg/dL glucose rise
  • Glucose >400 mg/dL → Na⁺ decreases 2.4 mEq/L per 100 mg/dL glucose rise

Clinical Features

Acute
Cerebral oedema from hypo-osmolality: nausea, lethargy, seizures, coma
Chronic
Milder symptoms due to osmotic adaptation (loss of K⁺, Na⁺, organic solutes: myoinositol, amino acids)

Treatment

🔴 Severe (<125 mEq/L or <15 mEq from reference)
  • 1–2 mL/kg of 3% hypertonic saline changes Na⁺ by ~1 mEq/L
  • Limit rise to 10 mEq/L first 24h and 18 mEq/L first 48h
  • Hypovolaemic: treat with hypertonic fluid within 10 mEq/L of patient's Na⁺ until euvolaemic → remove ADH stimulus
  • Hypertonic (glucose-driven): remove the osmotic substance → resolves hyponatraemia
🟢 Chronic / Asymptomatic
  • Slow correction to avoid osmotic demyelination syndrome
  • Mild ≥130 mmol/L: often self-corrects by treating underlying disease
  • Monitor serum Na⁺ frequently
⚠️ Osmotic Demyelination Syndrome
Risk with rapid correction of chronic hyponatraemia → damage to myelin & oligodendrocytes → pons, thalamus, cerebrum, cerebellum affected. Signs: abnormal mentation, paralysis (2–7 days after change in Na⁺)
📝 KH Notes — Hyponatremia
  • Hyponatremia vs hypernatremia: strong association with outcome — not causality
  • K⁺ depends on distal renal flow; if prolonged ↓ GFR, UNa not accurate
  • Always abnormal if patient is hyponatraemic, thirsty, and drinking — suggests low ECV
  • Tolvaptan: helps treat hyponatraemia — very expensive

🔴Hypernatremia

Core Mechanism
↑ ECF tonicity → water moves OUT of cells (neurons least tolerant) → cell shrinkage
Less common than hyponatraemia — intense thirst is normally protective

Classification by Volume Status

NORMOVOLEMIA — Pure Water Loss
Measure urine FENa / solute clearance
  • Renal: Diabetes insipidus (Central / Nephrogenic)
  • Extrarenal: Inadequate water access, hypodipsia
HYPOVOLEMIA — Hypotonic Fluid Loss
Measure urine free water clearance
  • Renal: Chronic kidney failure, non-oliguric AKI, osmotic/chemical diuresis
  • GI loss, diarrhoea, vomiting, third space loss
  • Fever, hyperventilation, cutaneous fluid loss
HYPERVOLEMIA — Sodium Gain
  • Salt poisoning
  • Hypertonic saline
  • Sodium bicarbonate
  • Hyperaldosteronism
  • Hyperadrenocorticism

Clinical Features

Neurological Signs (severity ≥180 mmol/L = severe)
Lethargy, weakness, seizures, coma — due to cerebral dehydration. Focal intracerebral & subarachnoid haemorrhages or demyelination possible. Chronic cases may show fewer symptoms due to osmotic adaptation.

Physiological Adaptation to Hypernatremia

Mins–Hours: CSF Compensation
  • Lowered interstitial hydraulic pressure draws CSF into brain interstitium
  • Brain cells increase uptake of Na & K from CSF → ↑ neuronal osmolality → water back into cells
Within 24h: Idiogenic Osmoles
Taurine Glutamine Glutamate Glycine Inositol Myoinositol Phosphocreatine Sorbitol

Form within neurons → ↑ intracellular osmolality → draw water back in. Full compensation: 2–7 days

Treatment

🔴 Acute (<48 hours) — Rapid Correction
  • 5% dextrose 3–6 mL/kg/hr OR 0.45% NaCl
  • Target: 1–2 mEq/L/hr within 24 hrs
  • If clinical signs: 5% dextrose at 7–10 mL/kg/h
  • Alternative hypotonic: up to 2–3 mEq/L/h for initial 2–3 hours
🟢 Chronic (>48 hours) — Slow Correction
  • Max safe correction: <0.5 mEq/L/hr
  • In absence of clinical signs: 10 mEq/L/day (24h) and 18 mEq/L (48h)
  • Avoid over-slow correction: <0.25 mEq/L/hr also dangerous
🟣 Specific Conditions
  • Post-op / overcorrection for hyponatremia: Give DDAVP ± free water
  • Central DI: DDAVP 1 drop (1.5–4 µL) conjunctival sac q8–12h, alternate eyes
  • Nephrogenic DI: Treat underlying cause; consider thiazides, NSAIDs, low Na⁺ diet
  • Sodium overload: Remove excess Na⁺; diuretics + hypotonic fluid replacement (e.g. D5W)
⚠️ If Hypernatremia Corrected Too Fast → Cerebral Oedema
Signs: obtundation, head pressing, coma, seizures
1. Stop free water infusion; measure plasma Na⁺
2. Mannitol 0.5–1 g/kg slow IV → if no improvement: 3–5 mL/kg hypertonic (7.2%) saline slow IV
*Rapid hypertonic saline can cause vasodilation
Rapid Correction Consequences
Rapid Onset Acute Hypernatremia OR Rapid Correction of Chronic Hyponatremia
↑ Plasma [Na⁺] rapidly
OSMOTIC DEMYELINATION + Intracranial Hemorrhage
Rapid Onset Acute Hyponatremia OR Rapid Correction of Chronic Hypernatremia
↓ Plasma [Na⁺] rapidly

🧪Urine Osmolality

Estimation from USG

Quick Estimate
UOsm ≈ Last two digits of USG × 28–30
Normal UOsm Ranges
  • Dogs: 800–2500 mOsm/kg
  • Cats: 600–3000 mOsm/kg

Isosthenuria

Plasma osmolality ≈ 300 mOsm/kg = USG ≈ 1.010
Isosthenuria range: USG 1.007–1.012
Kidney unable to concentrate or dilute → urine excreted at plasma osmolality
⚠️ Caveat
Large molecular weight substances (glucose, proteins, radioiodine contrast) will increase USG and overestimate osmolality. Use osmometer for accurate measurement.

Clinical Significance

FindingInterpretation
UOsm >1200 mOsm/kg, USG >1.035Adequate renal concentrating function
UOsm determines ADH activityDifferentiates prerenal from renal azotaemia
Low UOsm (hypotonic)PUPD (DM, HAC), medullary washout, steroids, diuretics
UOsm + urine [Na⁺]Diagnosis of SIADH

Spot Check Formula (Formula 6)

Electrolyte Ratio Interpretation
(UNa + UK) / Serum Na

= 120/120 → Isotonic loss
= 160/120 → Losing hypertonic solution (with ADH secretion)
= 60/120 → 0.5 Hypotonic loss

🔢Key Formulas

Formula 1 — Calculated Osmolality
Posm (mOsm/kg) = 2[Na⁺] + [Glucose]/18 + [BUN]/2.8
Formula 2 — Calculated Effective Posm / Tonicity
Posm effective (mOsm/kg) = 2[Na⁺] + [Glucose]/18
Formula 3 — Osmol Gap
Osmol gap (mOsm/kg) = Measured Posm − Calculated Posm
Formula 4 — Na Deficit
Na deficit (mEq) = 0.6 × BW (kg) × ([Na⁺] normal − [Na⁺] patient)
Formula 5 — Change [Na⁺] / 1L Infusate
Δ[Na⁺] (mEq) = ([Na⁺] infusate + [K⁺] infusate − [Na⁺] patient) / (0.6 × BW(kg) + 1)
Formula 6 — Electrolyte-free Water Clearance
Vurine (mL) × (1 − ([Na⁺] urine + [K⁺] urine) / [Na⁺] serum)
Formula 7 — Water Deficit
Water deficit (L) = 0.6 × BW (kg) × ([Na⁺] patient / [Na⁺] normal − 1)
Units: [Na⁺], [K⁺] in mEq/L; [Glucose] in mg/dL; [BUN] in mg/dL; BW in kg; [K⁺] = potassium; [Na⁺] = sodium; Posm = plasma osmolality; Vurine = volume of urine
