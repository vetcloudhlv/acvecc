Chapter 32

Mechanical Ventilation — Core Concepts

Kate Hopper, BVSc, PhD, DACVECC

Key Points — Chapter 32
  • Main indications: severe hypoxemia despite O₂ supplementation, severe hypoventilation despite therapy, excessive respiratory effort
  • Goal: maintain acceptable arterial blood gas parameters with the least aggressive ventilator settings
  • Lung disease → more aggressive settings needed, poorer prognosis vs. neuromuscular disease
  • "Ideal" ventilator settings can only be determined by trial and error for each patient
🫁
Physiology of Positive Pressure Ventilation

The respiratory function of the lungs is to oxygenate arterial blood and remove CO₂ from venous blood. It is clinically useful to think of oxygenation and ventilation as two separate processes.

Oxygenation

Movement of O₂ from alveoli → pulmonary capillaries. Depends on surface area for gas exchange and integrity of the gas-exchange barrier.

Ventilation

Fresh gas movement into alveoli. Primary determinant of CO₂ elimination. Controlled via RR × tidal volume.

Intrathoracic Pressure

Spontaneous breathing: pleural pressure falls during inspiration (subatmospheric). PPV: positive airway pressures generate inspiratory flow — the reverse mechanism.

BOX 32.1 — Equation of Motion
Pvent + Pmuscles = Elastance × Volume + Resistance × Flow
Pvent = pressure generated by the ventilator
Pmuscles = pressure generated by inspiratory muscles
Note: Elastance = inverse of compliance
Resistance

Resistance = Driving Pressure ÷ Flow (Ohm's Law). Increased by airway collapse, narrowing, bronchoconstriction. The endotracheal tube is often the greatest source of resistance during MV.

Compliance

Compliance = ΔVolume / ΔPressure — a measure of lung distensibility. In ventilated patients: delivered VT / pressure required to generate VT. High compliance = large ΔV for small ΔP. Low compliance = high pressures needed for small ΔV. Most veterinary pulmonary diseases reduce compliance.

Phase Variables
Fig 32.2 — Pressure-Time Scalar: Phases of a Ventilator Breath

Control Variable

The one variable directly controlled per breath: pressure, volume, or flow. The other two become dependent variables determined by compliance and resistance.

Limit Variable

The parameter the breath cannot exceed during inspiration — but does NOT terminate the breath (unlike a cycle variable). e.g., pressure-limited in VC mode.

Cycle Variable

Determines termination of inspiratory flow. Most common is time (set by RR and I:E ratio). In PSV, a set reduction in flow rate is the cycle variable.

Trigger Variable

Initiates inspiration. Time (from set RR) in apneic patients. In spontaneously breathing patients: change in airway pressure or flow, requiring appropriate trigger sensitivity.

Baseline Variable

Controlled during exhalation (expiratory control variable). Usually airway pressure. If maintained above atmospheric during exhalation → PEEP.

⚙️
Ventilation Modes
Ventilation Mode Classification
Fig 32.3 — SIMV Pressure-Time Scalar

In SIMV, mandatory breaths are delivered at a set rate. Between cycles, the patient breathes spontaneously. If the patient triggers during the breath-cycle window → assist-controlled breath. If no effort by 90% of cycle time → controlled breath.

I:E Ratio and Respiratory Rate

Normal starting RR: 15–20 breaths/min. Standard I:E ratio: 1:2 (ensures full exhalation before next breath). If inspiratory time exceeds expiratory time → inverse I:E ratio (can cause breath stacking / intrinsic PEEP). As RR increases, expiratory time is sacrificed.

🎚️
Ventilator Settings

Key adjustable parameters: RR, VT, inspiratory pressure, inspiratory time, I:E ratio, trigger sensitivity, and PEEP. With volume-controlled ventilation, VT is preset; PIP is dependent. With pressure-controlled ventilation, airway pressure is preset; VT is dependent.

🔄
Positive End-Expiratory Pressure (PEEP)

PEEP maintains positive airway pressure during exhalation, preventing the lung from emptying completely — holding the lung at a higher volume and pressure.

Benefits of PEEP

Recruits collapsed alveoli · Prevents further alveolar collapse · Reduces ventilator-induced lung injury · Improves oxygenating efficiency · Prevents atelectasis

Harmful Effects of PEEP

Adds to total inspiratory pressure (PIP = set pressure + PEEP) · Maintains elevated intrathoracic pressure during exhalation → may compromise venous return → ↓ cardiac output

ℹ️
If PEEP = 5 cm H₂O and ventilator delivers 20 cm H₂O during inspiration → peak airway pressure = 25 cm H₂O. Always factor PEEP into total pressure calculations. Cardiovascular monitoring is essential with high PEEP levels.
🔔
Ventilator Alarms

Set patient-specific alarm values every time the ventilator is set up, and readjust when significant setting changes are made. Properly set alarms can be life-saving — detecting disconnections, apnea, circuit leaks, pneumothorax, and changes in patient status.

Indications for Mechanical Ventilation
Indications Decision Flow
1

Severe Hypoxemia

Cyanosis, PaO₂ <60 mmHg, or SpO₂ <90% despite O₂ therapy and treatment of primary disease. Note: venous PaO₂ cannot be used. FiO₂ >60% for >24–48 h → risk of O₂ toxicity → consider MV.

2

Severe Hypoventilation

PaCO₂ >60 mmHg unresponsive to therapy. Venous PCO₂ is a reliable surrogate in hemodynamically stable patients. Causes: neurologic/neuromuscular disease, anesthesia. Brain disease patients: consider MV if PaCO₂ >45 mmHg.

3

Excessive Respiratory Effort / Impending Fatigue

No definitive measure — requires clinical judgment. Intervene before respiratory arrest, even with acceptable blood gas values. Marked effort can lead to exhaustion and arrest.

4

Severe Hemodynamic Compromise (PPV indication)

Refractory to therapy. MV can decrease O₂ consumption and support left heart function. Anesthesia with opioids/benzodiazepines often feasible.

▶️
Initiation of MV & Goals
Initiation Steps

Before Connecting

Machine setup and monitoring required. Start with 100% O₂ until function confirmed. Test with artificial lung/rebreathing bag. Manual PPV available at all times for machine failure.

After Connecting

Observe chest for appropriate movement. Auscultate bilaterally — no bilateral sounds → suspect endobronchial intubation, retract ETT. Adjust if movements are excessive or inadequate.

Monitoring Tools

ECG · Pulse oximetry · ETCO₂ · Arterial blood pressure. Arterial blood gas (ABG) to verify monitors and guide settings. Without ABG: use physical exam + venous PCO₂ + pulse ox.

Goals of MV
TARGET BLOOD GAS VALUES
PaCO₂: 35–50 mmHg  |  PaO₂: 80–120 mmHg
Achieve with the LEAST aggressive ventilator settings possible.
Make ONE change at a time to accurately interpret the effect of each adjustment.
Record settings with concurrent ABG, ETCO₂, and SpO₂ values.
Managing CO₂

Alveolar minute ventilation = RR × (VT – dead space volume). Increases in dead space → ↓ effective alveolar ventilation → hypercapnia. If hypercapnia persists after ruling out equipment issues → increase RR and/or VT.

Managing Oxygenation

First priority after stabilization: reduce FiO₂ to ≤60% as soon as possible to reduce O₂ toxicity risk. Once FiO₂ ≤60%, consider reducing PEEP and peak inspired pressure if PaO₂ is significantly above target. In severe hypoxemia despite 100% O₂: increase PEEP, PIP, VT, and/or RR. Maintain sternal recumbency until stabilized.

⚠️
Complications of Mechanical Ventilation

Cardiovascular Compromise

Impairment of intrathoracic blood flow. Worse with cardiovascular instability or aggressive settings. Cardiovascular monitoring required for ALL ventilated patients.

Ventilator-Induced Lung Injury (VILI)

Volutrauma + repetitive alveolar opening/collapse are major causes. Reduce with lung-protective ventilation strategies.

Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia (VAP)

Prevent with aseptic airway procedures, intensive oral care, reducing gastric regurgitation. Monitor with airway cytology and culture/susceptibility testing.

Pneumothorax

Feared complication. More likely due to underlying lung disease than ventilator settings. Signs: acute ↓ oxygenation, ↑ PCO₂, ↓ chest wall movement and compliance, PVA. Can be rapidly fatal under PPV — tension pneumothorax requires emergency thoracostomy.

🔧
Troubleshooting the Ventilator Patient

Patient–ventilator dyssynchrony ("bucking the ventilator") = patient breathing against the machine. Causes hypoxemia, hypercapnia, hyperthermia, increased work of breathing. Requires a systematic approach.

Chapter 33

Mechanical Ventilation — Advanced Concepts

Kimberly Slensky, DVM, DACVECC & Deborah C. Silverstein, DVM, DACVECC

Key Points — Chapter 33
  • Advanced modes can optimize oxygenation and manage hypercarbia when standard modes are insufficient
  • Patient–ventilator asynchrony (PVA) is common (10–85%) and may be related to patient or ventilator variables
  • Lung protective strategies aim to limit ventilator-induced lung injury
  • APRV may reduce PVA via its inverse I:E ratio and allowance for unrestricted spontaneous breathing
  • PRVC automatically adjusts inspiratory pressure breath-by-breath to deliver a target VT
🔀
Patient–Ventilator Asynchrony (PVA)

PVA = mismatch between patient needs (flow, volume, time, pressure) and the ventilator-assisted breath. Rates of 10–85% reported. Diagnosed invasively (phrenic neurogram, esophageal balloon — gold standard) or non-invasively via waveform analysis (flow, volume, pressure scalars).

Patient Factors

Anxiety · Fever · Acidosis · Level of sedation · Underlying disease (ARDS, obstructive disease) · Airway issues (secretions, bronchospasm, ETT placement)

Ventilator Factors

Selected mode and settings · Machine-related issues (leaks) · Pleural space disease (pneumothorax)

Consequences of PVA

Patient discomfort · Cardiovascular instability · ↑ work of breathing · Impaired gas exchange · VILI · Prolonged need for MV

ℹ️
Common types of asynchrony: triggering, cycling, and flow asynchrony. Waveform analysis is the primary non-invasive tool. Optimize patient–ventilator interaction via monitoring of available waveform data.
🛡️
Lung Protective Ventilation

Strategy to limit alveolar overdistention contributing to VILI. Evidence base primarily from ARDS patients. ARDSNet (1998) proved clear mortality benefit of low VT ventilation (now standard of care in humans).

Low Tidal Volume

~4–6 ml/kg in people with ARDS. Reduces: volutrauma (overdistention/shearing), barotrauma (alveolar rupture → pneumothorax), biotrauma (inflammatory mediators from prolonged MV).

Limited Plateau Pressure

Target plateau pressure <30 cm H₂O. Monitor carefully, especially with volume-controlled modes.

PEEP Optimization

Improves oxygenation by recruiting alveoli, reducing VILI, decreasing shunt fraction. No definitive optimal PEEP strategy established — titration required.

Permissive Hypercapnia

Acceptable consequence of low VT ventilation. Tolerated as long as pH changes are gradual and oxygenation is maintained. Avoids alveolar overdistention and auto-PEEP.

⚠️
Severe hypercapnia is associated with higher mortality in ARDS patients. Higher minute ventilation may be necessary for those adversely affected by hypercarbia.

📉
Refractory Hypoxemia & PEEP Titration

Patients remaining hypoxemic despite 100% O₂ and lung-protective strategies. Prognosis poor (~60% mortality in humans). Adjunctive strategies: inhaled nitric oxide, HFOV, neuromuscular blockade, corticosteroids, prone positioning, ECLS. Veterinary data scarce.

PEEP Titration Strategies

Decremental PEEP Trial

Best compliance at a decremental (not incremental) PEEP trial is related to open-lung PEEP. Start high, decrease stepwise.

Compliance-Guided Titration

Find PEEP associated with highest compliance.

Oxygenation-Guided

Adjust PEEP to achieve best oxygenation.

Transpulmonary Pressure

Monitor transpulmonary pressure with esophageal balloon catheter.

Airway Pressure Release Ventilation (APRV)

First described in 1987. Time-cycled, pressure-controlled, intermittent mandatory ventilation mode with extreme inverse I:E ratios. Uses two pressure levels (Phigh and Plow), spending ~80–95% of time at Phigh. Allows unrestricted spontaneous breathing during Phigh.

Fig 33.1 — APRV Pressure-Time Curve

Benefits of APRV

Lung protective · ↑ alveolar surface area for gas exchange · Improved V/Q matching · Improved hemodynamics · ↓ need for sedation and neuromuscular blockade · Limits volutrauma and atelectrauma

Limitations / Controversies

Conflicting data vs. other lung-protective modes · Pediatric ARDS study showed 2× mortality with APRV · Role in veterinary patients undefined · Requires deep anesthesia ± paralytics for recruitment maneuvers

🤖
Pressure-Regulated Volume Control (PRVC / VC+)

Automatically adjusts breath-to-breath inspiratory pressure based on a set tidal volume and changing lung mechanics. Time- or patient-triggered, pressure-limited, time-cycled. Works in assist-control or SIMV.

How PRVC Works

Advantages

Adjusts to real-time changes in lung mechanics · Alerts clinician to ↑ airway pressures and ↓ VT · As compliance improves → pressure decreases automatically · Near-constant minute ventilation maintained

Potential Disadvantage

With ↓ compliance and set VT → airway pressures may increase, causing alveolar overdistention. Less of a concern when lung-protective tidal volumes and appropriate pressure limits are used.
