Chapter Summary

Urine electrolytes allow clinicians to assess whether the kidney's response to an electrolyte or acid-base disturbance is appropriate. The key principle: interpret the renal response in the context of the clinical abnormality — not in isolation.

Urine sodium and osmolality reflect RAAS and ADH activity respectively, aiding volume status assessment and dysnatremia evaluation. Urine chloride differentiates causes of metabolic alkalosis. The urine anion gap estimates ammonium excretion in metabolic acidosis. Urine potassium, calcium, and magnesium help evaluate dyskalemia, urolithiasis, and hypomagnesemia.

All results must be interpreted alongside history, physical examination, concurrent medications, and other laboratory data. Many cutoffs derive from human medicine and may not translate directly to small animals.

Key Points from the Chapter
  • Urine electrolytes are best interpreted by evaluation of the renal response to a specific abnormality
  • Urine sodium and osmolality are helpful in assessment of volume status and evaluation of dysnatremia
  • Urine chloride may aid in differentiation of causes of metabolic alkalosis; urine anion gap may be useful in metabolic acidosis
  • Urine potassium, calcium, and magnesium can be helpful in evaluating dyskalemia, calcium-based urolithiasis, and hypomagnesemia
  • Useful interpretation requires incorporation of history, physical examination, and other laboratory data alongside knowledge of limitations
01
Urine Sodium & Osmolality

The kidneys maintain total body fluid volume and osmolality as part of normal homeostasis. Extracellular fluid volume is determined by total body sodium content, while osmolality is determined by water content.

U[Na⁺] → RAAS Activity
  • < 20 mEq/L → RAAS activated; decreased ECV
  • > 40 mEq/L → RAAS not active; fluid replete (assumes normal renal function)
  • 20–40 mEq/L: gray zone; some human sources use 30–40 as low cutoff
Urine Osmolality → ADH Activity
  • > 200 mOsm/kg → ADH is active
  • Dogs: 369–2416 mOsm/kg
  • Cats: up to 3000 mOsm/kg
  • Estimate in dogs: last 2 digits of USG × 36

Fractional Excretion of Sodium (FENa)

FENa removes the influence of urine concentration by indexing urine sodium to urine creatinine (excreted at a nearly constant rate). Used to differentiate prerenal from renal AKI.

Formula
FENa = (U[Na⁺] × Blood Cr) ÷ (Blood [Na⁺] × Urine Cr) × 100
FENa < 1% → kidneys retaining Na⁺ (prerenal / early AKI) · FENa > 2% → tubular damage, established renal AKI
Important Caveat
Diuretics artificially raise FENa even in hypovolemic patients. In diuretic-treated patients, FEUrea (cutoff < 40% transient, > 40% established AKI) may be a better alternative — though not yet validated in small animals.

Electrolyte-Free Water Clearance (EFWC)

Used to assess whether the kidney is retaining or excreting free water — key in evaluating dysnatremia.

Formula
EFWC = Urine volume × [ 1 − (U[Na⁺] + U[K⁺]) ÷ Blood [Na⁺] ]
Positive = excreting free water · Negative = retaining free water · Note: glucosuria invalidates this formula
Clinical Pearl
Comparing the ratio of plasma [Na⁺] to combined U[Na⁺] + U[K⁺] is a simple way to judge whether the kidney is retaining or excreting free water. This is more reliable than urine osmolality alone in most circumstances.

Causes of Low U[Na⁺] Without True Hypovolemia

Decreased ECV with RAAS activation (and non-osmotic ADH release) also occurs in heart failure and hypoalbuminemia, despite normal or elevated total body water. A low U[Na⁺] may also be found in normovolemic polyuric patients where dilution reduces sodium concentration even when sodium content is appropriate.

02
Urine Chloride

The majority of filtered chloride is reabsorbed in the proximal tubule alongside water, with further reabsorption in the loop of Henle and distal nephron. Chloride has a reciprocal relationship with bicarbonate — chloride depletion leads to a compensatory rise in bicarbonate, causing metabolic alkalosis.

When to Use It

Urine chloride is most useful in the evaluation of metabolic alkalosis — specifically to distinguish chloride-responsive from chloride-resistant causes.

U[Cl⁻] Classification Common Causes Treatment Principle
< 25 mEq/L Chloride-responsive Vomiting (HCl loss), historical diuretic use, GI suctioning Administer chloride (0.9% NaCl); alkalosis resolves
> 40 mEq/L Chloride-resistant Primary/secondary hyperaldosteronism, salt-wasting tubular disorders, severe hypokalemia, current diuretic use Treat underlying cause; saline alone will not resolve
Small Animal Note
Urine chloride calculations may be less useful in small animal medicine because causes like surreptitious vomiting or diuretic use (classic human scenarios) are less common in our patients. Still important to know for board examinations.
03
Urine Potassium

Potassium is freely filtered through the glomerulus; ~90% is reabsorbed in the proximal tubule and thick ascending limb. The distal nephron is where regulated secretion or reabsorption occurs, largely under aldosterone control. Multiple methods exist for evaluating urinary potassium.

Four Evaluation Methods Compared

MethodFormulaHypokalemia ThresholdsHyperkalemia ThresholdsKey Notes
Spot U[K⁺] Direct measurement <15 = extrarenal · >40 = renal wasting — Simple but unreliable; doesn't account for water reabsorption
FEK (U[K⁺] × PCr) ÷ (P[K⁺] × UCr) × 100 <4–6% = nonrenal · >6% = renal loss — Varies with GFR; useful in AKI monitoring
TTKG (U[K⁺] × Plasma Osm) ÷ (P[K⁺] × Urine Osm) <3 = extrarenal · >3 = renal <7 = ↓ renal excretion · >7 = extrarenal Requires U[Na⁺] >25 AND Uosm ≥ Plasma Osm. Physiologic assumptions questioned
UK:UCr U[K⁺] ÷ UCr (mmol/L) — see formula box for mg/dL conversion <2.5 = nonrenal · >2.5 = renal wasting <10 = inappropriate · >20 = appropriate/extrarenal Preferred over TTKG; no restrictive assumptions; applies regardless of U[Na⁺] or osmolality
UK:UCr Conversion (when creatinine in mg/dL)
UK:UCr = U[K⁺] × 1000 ÷ (Urine Cr [mg/dL] × 88)
Use same reference ranges regardless of which unit conversion is applied · Creatinine usually measured in mmol/L (not μmol/L) in SI units
TTKG Prerequisites
TTKG is only valid when: (1) U[Na⁺] > 25 mEq/L — ensures sodium delivery to principal cells is not rate-limiting for K⁺ secretion, AND (2) Urine osmolality ≥ Plasma osmolality — active ADH required for optimal K⁺ secretion. If either condition fails, use UK:UCr instead.

Evaluating Hypokalemia — Key Differential Logic

Extrarenal Loss (U[K⁺] < 15, FEK < 4–6%)
  • GI loss (vomiting, diarrhea)
  • Third space loss
  • Cutaneous loss (rare)
  • Intracellular translocation (alkalosis, insulin)
  • Pseudohypokalemia
Renal Loss — then check U[Na⁺]
U[Na⁺] < 20:
  • Primary hyperaldosteronism
  • Primary tubular defect
  • Hyperadrenocorticism
  • Amphotericin B
U[Na⁺] > 40:
  • Intrinsic renal injury, RTA
  • Drugs (diuretics, penicillins)
  • Hypomagnesemia

Evaluating Hyperkalemia — Key Causes of Decreased Renal Excretion (TTKG < 7)

When TTKG < 7 or UK:UCr < 10 in a hyperkalemic patient, decreased renal potassium excretion is implicated. Main causes include: decreased aldosterone secretion (hypoadrenocorticism), decreased response to aldosterone, decreased delivery of sodium and water to distal tubule, kidney disease, and post-renal causes (urethral/ureteral obstruction, uroabdomen).

AKI Monitoring Pearl
The combined use of FENa and FEK may be informative: decreasing FENa + increasing FEK suggests RAAS activation, indicating renal stress and risk of AKI development. FEK increases during AKI onset and decreases on recovery, making it useful for serial monitoring.
04
Urine Anion Gap (UAG)

The UAG is the primary tool for evaluating hyperchloremic (non-anion gap) metabolic acidosis. It serves as a rough estimate of urinary ammonium (NH₄⁺) concentration — the kidney's main mechanism for excreting excess acid.

Formula
UAG = U[Na⁺] + U[K⁺] − U[Cl⁻]
Urine bicarbonate is excluded because it is present in only very small amounts at urine pH < 6.6

Interpretation Logic

In metabolic acidosis, kidneys should increase NH₄⁺ excretion. NH₄⁺ is excreted alongside Cl⁻, raising U[Cl⁻] and making the UAG more negative. If the kidney cannot secrete H⁺ (distal RTA), NH₄⁺ cannot increase → UAG stays positive.

UAG ValueNH₄⁺ ExcretionRenal ResponseDiagnoses to Consider
Negative (−20 to −50) Appropriately increased Normal — kidney is working GI HCO₃⁻ loss (diarrhea), dilutional acidosis — extrarenal cause
Positive or near zero Inappropriately low Abnormal — kidney is failing to excrete acid Distal (Type 1) RTA, Type 4 RTA (hyperkalemia + acidosis), generalized kidney disease
Key Limitations of UAG
UAG assumes NH₄⁺ is excreted with Cl⁻ — this is invalid if urine contains ketones (DKA → false-positive UAG mimicking RTA), bicarbonate, or other anionic compounds. Also unreliable in generalized kidney disease. Dietary variation adds further inter-individual variability.
RTA Diagnostic Clue
Failure to acidify urine below pH 6.0 in the face of systemic metabolic acidosis should raise suspicion for distal (Type 1) RTA — even without a formal UAG calculation — provided urinary tract infection with urease-producing organisms has been excluded.
05
Miscellaneous: Mg²⁺ & Ca²⁺

Urine Magnesium (FEMg)

The kidney reabsorbs approximately 95% of filtered magnesium. A FEMg of ~1% has been demonstrated in healthy dogs. FEMg is used to determine whether hypomagnesemia is of renal or extrarenal origin.

FEMg Formula
FEMg = 100 × (U[Mg²⁺] × Blood Cr) ÷ (0.7 × Blood [Mg²⁺] × Urine Cr)
The 0.7 factor accounts for ~70% of serum magnesium being unbound and available for glomerular filtration · Values based on total (not ionized) magnesium
FEMgInterpretationCauses
< 2% Extrarenal loss — kidney appropriately conserving Mg²⁺ GI loss, decreased intake, translocation, redistribution
> 2–4% Renal wasting — kidney inappropriately losing Mg²⁺ Loop diuretics, aminoglycosides, cisplatin, CKD, hypercalcemia

Urine Calcium (FECa)

FECa = (U[Ca²⁺] × Blood Cr) ÷ (Blood [Ca²⁺] × Urine Cr) × 100

Hypercalciuria is considered important in calcium oxalate urolithiasis pathogenesis. Higher urine Ca:Cr ratios have been reported in predisposed dog breeds (e.g., miniature schnauzers), though substantial overlap with unaffected dogs limits clinical utility. Hydrochlorothiazide promotes calcium uptake in the thick ascending limb, decreasing urinary calcium — efficacy can be monitored with serial U[Ca²⁺] measurements.

Urine Mg:Cr
Spot urine magnesium-to-creatinine ratios correlate reasonably well with 24-hour urine Mg²⁺ concentrations in people, making them a practical alternative to timed collections.
06
Limitations to Know
Human-Derived Cutoffs Many threshold values (FENa 1–2%, FEK 4–6%, UK:UCr 2.5) originate from human medicine. Direct extrapolation to dogs and cats may not be valid and has not been fully validated.
Spot Sample Variability Single-timepoint measurements may not correlate well with 24-hour excretion. Fractional excretion indices partially correct for concentration effects, but 24-hour collections remain the gold standard.
Diuretic Interference Diuretics raise U[Na⁺] and FENa even in hypovolemic patients by blocking renal sodium reabsorption. FEUrea is a better alternative in these patients, though not yet validated in small animals.
TTKG Assumptions Requires U[Na⁺] > 25 mEq/L AND Uosm ≥ Plasma Osm. If either fails, TTKG is invalid. UK:UCr is now preferred as it lacks these restrictions.
FENa in AKI Many AKI patients with intrinsic renal injury still have FENa < 1%. Behavior of urine electrolytes differs little between transient and persistent AKI, limiting therapeutic and prognostic relevance.
Point-of-Care Accuracy Discrepancy between central lab and bedside analyzers for U[K⁺] averaged ~19.7 mmol/L in one human study. U[K⁺] often exceeds analyzer upper range; samples must be diluted. Consult manufacturer for correction factors.
Sample Handling Urine electrolytes are stable for up to 48 hours at various temperatures; osmolality may change sooner. Note storage time when submitting to external laboratories.
IV Fluids & Medications Active IV fluid therapy alters sodium excretion, tubular flow rate, and urine electrolyte concentrations. Always document concurrent therapies when interpreting results.
